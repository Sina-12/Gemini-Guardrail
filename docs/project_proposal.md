- What kind of text is it? What language is it in? What is the genre/register? What are the texts about? Who wrote them? How long are the documents, generally? 
  - Our corpus will consist of user-generated Reddit comments that express strong opinions and try to persuade other users. The language is English. The genre is online discussion, and the register is informal and conversational, but it often includes structured argumentation, because users respond directly to each other and defend their positions. The topics depend on the subreddit, but they generally involve debates about social issues, personal decisions, unpopular opinions, or requests for judgement, so the texts are naturally stance-driven and evaluative. The texts are written by Reddit users, who are typically anonymous or use pseudonyms, which makes the corpus useful for studying language without strong real-world identity signals. A single document in our corpus will usually be one comment. Most comments are short to medium length, often between one and five sentences, but longer argumentative replies also occur, especially when users provide evidence or counter-arguments.
- Will you be targeting a specific kind of text among those available on the site? If so, how will you be filtering the texts to just the kind you want? Is there enough data there to create a "Brown-sized" corpus? (Note, if you really like your corpus idea but it falls far short of "Brown-sized", discuss it with your mentor before your project proposal)
  - We will target comments that are clearly argumentative or opinionated rather than general conversation. To do this, we will collect data from subreddits where persuasion and stance are central to the interaction, and we will filter for posts that have replies, because those tend to contain actual discourse and not just isolated statements. We can also filter out very short comments that do not contain enough linguistic material for analysis. Based on initial exploration, these communities contain a very large number of comments, so reaching a Brown-sized corpus is realistic. Since the average comment contains dozens of tokens, collecting tens of thousands of comments will allow us to reach the one-million-word range.
  
- Is there any structure to the corpus you are building (e.g. discussion threads)? Any metadata (e.g. related to author identity)?
  - Yes. The corpus has a clear hierarchical structure because Reddit discussions are organized as threads. Each comment has a parent comment, which allows us to reconstruct the reply tree and study discourse in context rather than as isolated texts. This is useful for analyzing how arguments develop and how stance is expressed in interaction. We will also keep available metadata such as subreddit name, comment score, timestamp, comment depth in the thread, and anonymized author name. Even though the authors are pseudonymous, this metadata still allows us to study patterns like how language changes depending on interactional position or community.
  
- What do you have in mind for your annotation of this corpus? (this does not have to be your final choice of annotation, but you should have an idea before you start collecting data)
  - Our main idea is to annotate stance in relation to the original post or the parent comment, for example whether a comment supports, rejects, or is neutral toward the previous claim. This connects to tasks we have seen in the program such as document-level and discourse-level annotation. If time allows, we may also add a second layer that captures simple argument functions such as claim, evidence, or rebuttal. Since full-corpus annotation is not realistic, we will manually annotate a representative sample of around one thousand instances and use clear guidelines so that the labels are consistent.
  
- In what format are you going to store the corpus and any associated metadata? (JSON? txt? Database?)  
  - We plan to store the corpus in JSON format, with one document per entry. This allows us to keep the text together with its metadata and its position in the thread structure. The annotations will either be stored as additional fields in the JSON or in a separate stand-off format so that the raw text is preserved. This format is flexible, easy to process with Python, and suitable for building a searchable web interface.
  
- What makes this corpus potentially of interest? What could it be used for? (Think broadly a lot of corpora have secondary uses beyond the primary annotation.)
  - This corpus is interesting because it captures naturally occurring persuasive language in an interactive setting, which is something that is not well represented in more traditional balanced corpora. The thread structure and the stance annotation make it useful for studying discourse, argumentation, and evaluation in context. Beyond this course, it could be used to train and evaluate models for stance detection, argument mining, sentiment in dialogue, or conversational NLP more broadly. It could also support linguistic research on how people express disagreement, hedge their claims, or use evidence in informal online genres. Because we are preserving rich metadata and interactional structure, the corpus can be reused for both corpus linguistics and machine learning tasks.
